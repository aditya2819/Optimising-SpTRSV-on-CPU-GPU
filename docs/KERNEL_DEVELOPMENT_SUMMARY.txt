Kernel Development Summary
==========================

Purpose
-------
This document summarizes the kernel-development path on both CPU and GPU:
- what kernel was added
- what profiling or benchmark signal motivated it
- what decision was taken next

The focus is on the reasoning trail from baseline to final active kernels.


CPU Side
========

CPU Goal
--------
Build a clean sparse triangular solve benchmark that compares custom CPU kernels
against MKL, while keeping the timed region focused on the solve call itself.

Initial baseline
----------------
Kernel:
- Basic

What it is:
- serial CSR lower-triangular forward solve

Why it was the starting point:
- simplest custom CPU baseline
- easiest implementation to verify against residual checks and MKL

What the first benchmark signal showed:
- Basic was already very strong on the real matrix set
- it consistently beat MKL on the tested platform

Decision after the baseline:
- keep Basic as the reference custom kernel
- only pursue lightweight optimizations that had a chance to improve the inner loop


BranchReduced
-------------
Kernel:
- BranchReduced

What changed:
- diagonal handling was separated from the main accumulation loop
- the hot loop avoided the repeated branch pattern in Basic

Why it was added:
- the first CPU-side hypothesis was that a serial micro-optimization might help
  more than heavier restructuring

What profiling and benchmarking showed:
- correctness remained good
- timing on real matrices did not beat Basic consistently
- on Explorer, ASIC_100k showed BranchReduced slower than Basic

Decision:
- keep the kernel in the benchmark as an experiment
- do not promote it as the preferred CPU kernel


Unrolled
--------
Kernel:
- Unrolled

What changed:
- kept the serial structure
- used the branch-reduced structure
- unrolled the inner accumulation loop

Why it was added:
- after BranchReduced, the next reasonable serial experiment was a low-risk
  instruction-level optimization

What profiling and benchmarking showed:
- correctness remained good
- the profile shape remained similar to Basic
- any benefit was small and not strong enough to replace Basic as the final choice

Decision:
- keep it as an explored serial variant
- do not replace Basic with it


BasicOmp
--------
Kernel:
- BasicOmp

What changed:
- dependency levels were computed
- rows in the same level were processed in parallel with OpenMP

Why it was added:
- test whether matrix-level parallelism on CPU could outperform the serial solve

What profiling and benchmarking showed:
- Explorer VTune runs on ASIC_100k showed visible OpenMP runtime overhead
- the profile explicitly showed `__kmp_api_GOMP_parallel`
- BasicOmp was much slower than Basic on representative matrices

Decision:
- do not treat BasicOmp as the final CPU kernel
- only continue OpenMP exploration to confirm whether overhead could be reduced


GuidedOmp
---------
Kernel:
- GuidedOmp

What changed:
- kept the level-scheduled OpenMP structure
- changed scheduling to guided to reduce imbalance on irregular levels

Why it was added:
- if OpenMP was losing due to poor within-level balance, guided scheduling was the
  lightest experiment to try next

What profiling and benchmarking showed:
- still slower than Basic across the real sweep
- among OpenMP variants it was less bad than some others, but still not competitive

Decision:
- keep as an explored variant
- continue only as part of the OpenMP comparison set, not as a leading candidate


BranchReducedOmp
----------------
Kernel:
- BranchReducedOmp

What changed:
- used the BranchReduced inner row kernel inside the OpenMP level schedule

Why it was added:
- test whether OpenMP overhead plus row-kernel overhead together could be improved

What profiling and benchmarking showed:
- no consistent win over BasicOmp
- still slower than Basic on the real sweep

Decision:
- retain only as an experimental OpenMP branch


UnrolledOmp
-----------
Kernel:
- UnrolledOmp

What changed:
- used the Unrolled row kernel inside the OpenMP level schedule

Why it was added:
- test whether the better serial inner loop could rescue the OpenMP path

What profiling and benchmarking showed:
- no strong improvement over BasicOmp
- still clearly behind Basic on real matrices

Decision:
- keep only as explored evidence
- not selected as a final CPU kernel


GuidedUnrolledOmp
-----------------
Kernel:
- GuidedUnrolledOmp

What changed:
- combined guided OpenMP scheduling with the unrolled row kernel

Why it was added:
- this was the strongest remaining OpenMP combination worth testing

What profiling and benchmarking showed:
- still slower than Basic on the full real sweep
- OpenMP-side overhead remained too large relative to useful work

Decision:
- conclude that the OpenMP branch did not justify further investment for this
  project’s final CPU result


CPU Profiling Summary
---------------------
What was profiled:
- full real sweep on Explorer
- individual matrix runs, especially ASIC_100k
- additional focused runs after BranchReduced and Unrolled were added

First whole-sweep VTune result:
- the first `real` sweep profile on Explorer showed that the process-level cost
  was dominated by Matrix Market parsing and setup rather than the solve body
- representative numbers from the full sweep:
  - elapsed time about `24.159s`
  - CPU time about `22.900s`
  - effective time about `22.530s`
  - effective CPU utilization only about `0.928 / 28`

Top hotspots in the whole-sweep run:
- `std::istream::_M_extract<double>`
  - about `5.736s`
  - about `25.0%`
- `std::istream::operator>>`
  - about `5.400s`
  - about `23.6%`
- `loadMatrixMarketAsLowerTriangular(...)`
  - about `1.612s`
  - about `7.0%`
- `std::basic_ios::init`
  - about `1.461s`
  - about `6.4%`
- `std::getline(...)`
  - about `1.381s`
  - about `6.0%`

What that meant:
- the full sweep was useful for understanding end-to-end workflow cost
- but it was not the right profile to use for choosing between kernel bodies

Decision after the whole-sweep profile:
- move from full-sweep profiling to one-matrix profiling
- use focused runs to understand the custom kernels more directly

One-matrix VTune result on ASIC_100k, Basic:
- elapsed time about `1.787s`
- CPU time about `0.800s`
- effective CPU utilization about `0.828 / 28`

Top hotspots:
- `std::istream::_M_extract<double>`
  - about `53.8%`
- `std::istream::operator>>`
  - about `20.0%`
- `loadMatrixMarketAsLowerTriangular(...)`
  - about `6.2%`

One-matrix VTune result on web-Stanford, Basic:
- elapsed time about `1.858s`
- CPU time about `0.890s`
- effective CPU utilization about `0.841 / 28`

Top hotspots:
- `std::istream::operator>>`
  - about `36.0%`
- `std::basic_ios::init`
  - about `14.6%`
- `loadMatrixMarketAsLowerTriangular(...)`
  - about `11.3%`

What repeated one-matrix VTune runs showed:
- even after switching to `real-one`, the process-level profile was still heavily
  influenced by matrix loading and parsing
- this was true across multiple real matrices, not only ASIC_100k
- that made VTune more useful as a direction-checking tool than as a pure
  instruction-level kernel microscope

OpenMP-specific VTune signal:
- `BasicOmp` introduced visible OpenMP runtime overhead
- the profile showed `__kmp_api_GOMP_parallel`
- effective utilization still remained low relative to available cores

What that meant:
- the OpenMP path was not losing only because of poor threshold choices
- there was a real coordination/runtime cost in the level-scheduled parallel path

Decision after the OpenMP-focused profiling:
- continue only a limited OpenMP exploration
- use the OpenMP branch mainly to confirm whether the overhead problem was
  structural

BranchReduced and Unrolled follow-up profiling:
- their profiles remained broadly similar to Basic
- no major hotspot disappeared
- no strong compute-side signal suggested a decisive replacement for Basic

Decision after the serial follow-up profiling:
- keep BranchReduced and Unrolled documented as explored variants
- retain Basic as the preferred CPU kernel

Important interpretation:
- the benchmark timings themselves were still valid because the solve call was
  the timed region
- the profiling runs were less clean as kernel-only profiles because VTune sees
  the whole process
- VTune was therefore used mainly to identify bad directions and bottlenecks in
  surrounding overhead, not to justify replacing Basic with another CPU kernel

Final CPU decision
------------------
- Basic remained the final custom CPU kernel
- BranchReduced and Unrolled were explored but did not provide a decisive upgrade
- all OpenMP variants were slower on the real sweep
- MKL remained the library baseline


GPU Side
========

GPU Goal
--------
Start from a correctness-first GPU baseline and build toward stronger custom
 kernels that reduce launch overhead, improve scheduling, and stay competitive
against cuSPARSE.

Initial clean baseline
----------------------
Paths kept at the start:
- CPU reference solver
- naive GPU kernel
- cuSPARSE baseline

Why:
- establish correctness first
- make every later improvement easy to compare against


Naive
-----
Kernel:
- naive

What it was:
- essentially a correctness baseline with no useful GPU parallelism

What we learned:
- correct
- far too slow to be a serious custom kernel

Decision:
- use only as a baseline
- move immediately to kernels that expose parallelism inside a row


Coalesced
---------
Kernel:
- coalesced

What changed:
- one CUDA block cooperates on a row
- CSR reads are organized more coalescently
- shared-memory reduction is used for the partial sums

Why it was added:
- improve memory behavior and intra-row parallelism without changing the overall
  solve structure too aggressively

What benchmarking showed:
- immediate major improvement over Naive
- became the first strong custom GPU kernel
- remained a durable reference kernel throughout later work

Decision:
- keep Coalesced as a main comparison kernel
- use it as a yardstick for later adaptive and family-aware designs


ThreadRow, WarpRow, BlockRow
----------------------------
Kernels:
- thread
- warp
- block

What changed:
- ThreadRow:
  - one thread per row
- WarpRow:
  - one warp per row
- BlockRow:
  - one block per row

Why they were added:
- understand how different row sizes behave under different execution granularities
- build components for later adaptive dispatch

What benchmarking showed:
- ThreadRow improved on Naive but was still weak
- WarpRow was stronger than ThreadRow for medium rows
- BlockRow only became useful for larger rows
- all three still suffered from excessive launch overhead when used row-by-row

Decision:
- do not treat any one of them as the final kernel
- use them as building blocks for an adaptive dispatcher


AdaptiveRow
-----------
Kernel:
- adaptive

What changed:
- dispatch among ThreadRow, WarpRow, and BlockRow depending on row size

Why it was added:
- no single row-specialized kernel won across all row widths

What profiling showed:
- Nsight revealed the real bottleneck was not just threshold choice
- the real problem was the number of launches
- representative launch counts:
  - old AdaptiveRow at n=8192: about 32780 launches
  - old AdaptiveRow at n=32768: about 131084 launches

Decision:
- threshold tuning alone was not enough
- the next step had to reduce launch count structurally


RowBucketed
-----------
Kernel:
- rowbucketed

What changed:
- rows were grouped into thread / warp / block buckets
- contiguous runs were compressed into segments
- launches were issued per segment instead of per row

Why it was added:
- preserve row-shape specialization
- eliminate the row-by-row launch storm exposed by Nsight

What profiling and benchmarking showed:
- launch count dropped dramatically
- RowBucketed became much stronger than AdaptiveRow
- it was one of the biggest structural gains after Coalesced

Decision:
- keep RowBucketed as a main custom GPU kernel
- after launch overhead was reduced, investigate matrix-level scheduling next


LevelAware
----------
Kernel:
- levelaware

What changed:
- dependency levels were computed on the host
- rows in the same level were organized into buckets and processed together

Why it was added:
- RowBucketed reduced launch overhead, but profiling showed the remaining problem
  was matrix-wide execution efficiency and idle waiting

What benchmarking and profiling showed:
- structurally correct and promising on matrices with wider dependency frontiers
- not a universal winner
- worked best on cases like wide-level or moderately irregular matrices
- still had avoidable per-level overhead on other families

Decision:
- do not make LevelAware the universal answer
- specialize the level-aware idea by family


RandomAware
-----------
Kernel:
- randomaware

What changed:
- built from the LevelAware idea
- reused device row-list buffers across levels
- used separate CUDA streams within a level
- retuned thresholds for random-like structure

Why it was added:
- generic LevelAware on random matrices was losing to avoidable host-side
  orchestration and allocation churn

What profiling showed:
- cudaMalloc/cudaFree counts dropped sharply compared with generic LevelAware
- launch count also dropped

What benchmarking showed:
- RandomAware was much stronger on random and irregular families

Decision:
- keep RandomAware as a key specialized kernel


PowerLawAware
-------------
Kernel:
- powerlawaware

What changed:
- level-aware / bucket-aware design specialized for power-law structure

Why it was added:
- graph-like and power-law matrices behaved differently from random irregular ones
- the row-width mix and frontier behavior suggested a different specialization

What benchmarking showed:
- PowerLawAware became one of the most frequent winners on graph-style real matrices
- often outperformed generic alternatives on large irregular real cases

Decision:
- keep as a major specialized custom kernel


BandedAware
-----------
Kernel:
- bandedaware

What changed:
- specialization intended for banded or near-banded structure

Why it was added:
- the family analysis showed banded matrices needed a different path than random
  or power-law matrices

What benchmarking showed:
- useful for structure-specific routing
- not a universal winner

Decision:
- keep as a selectable specialization


RegularAware
------------
Kernel:
- regularaware

What changed:
- specialization for more regular row-structure cases

Why it was added:
- some matrices were not random enough for RandomAware and not graph-like enough
  for PowerLawAware

What benchmarking showed:
- useful in the family-based routing set
- more of a routing target than a dominant global winner

Decision:
- keep as a supported specialization


BlockAware
----------
Kernel:
- blockaware

What changed:
- heavier block-oriented execution path for matrices with block-like structure

Why it was added:
- some matrices had denser local row patterns that could benefit from larger
  block-wide work

What benchmarking showed:
- useful for completeness in the family-aware set
- not one of the dominant overall winners

Decision:
- keep as a specialist kernel, not the main default


FamilyAware
-----------
Kernel:
- familyaware

What changed:
- hand-coded routing from matrix features/families to a selected specialized kernel

Why it was added:
- once several specialized kernels existed, there needed to be a structured way
  to route matrices without manual selection

What benchmarking showed:
- often selected strong kernels
- performed well on the curated family set
- served as the main rule-based adaptive baseline

Decision:
- keep FamilyAware as the rule-based selector baseline


LearnedAware
------------
Kernel:
- learnedaware

What changed:
- replaced hand-coded family routing with a learned selector
- uses matrix features to choose among the specialized kernels

Why it was added:
- test whether a learned selector could match or improve on manual routing

What benchmarking showed:
- competitive with FamilyAware
- not always the winner on every platform
- selector quality depended on the retained weights and the hardware used

Decision:
- keep LearnedAware as the learned selector path
- keep the final benchmark weights stable instead of promoting overfit retraining


cuSPARSE baseline
-----------------
Kernel:
- cusparse

Role:
- library baseline on GPU

Important benchmark decision:
- extra helper modes such as `learnedaware-solve`, custom `-persist` modes,
  `cusparse-solve`, and `cusparse-persist` were eventually removed from the main
  benchmark interface so the final comparison stayed cleaner:
  - custom kernels vs normal cuSPARSE

Why that mattered:
- it avoided mixing persistent steady-state paths with normal one-shot paths
- it made the CPU/GPU methodology more consistent


GPU Profiling Summary
---------------------
What profiling showed at each stage:

1. AdaptiveRow:
- too many launches
- the dominant issue was launch overhead

2. RowBucketed:
- launch count dropped sharply
- after that, the remaining bottleneck shifted toward total kernel completion and
  matrix-wide scheduling

3. LevelAware and family-aware kernels:
- family-specific structure mattered
- wide-frontier and irregular families benefited from different specialized paths

4. Later full-process Nsight runs:
- one-shot runs still showed substantial API/setup cost
- `cudaMalloc` and `cudaDeviceSynchronize` remained very visible in summaries
- that reinforced the need to compare like with like in the final benchmark modes

Overall Conclusions
===================

CPU
---
- The serial Basic kernel remained the best final CPU custom kernel.
- Serial micro-optimizations were explored carefully but did not produce a
  decisive replacement.
- OpenMP variants were useful to study, but their runtime overhead outweighed
  their benefit on the project benchmark set.

GPU
---
- The GPU path improved through clear structural steps:
  - correctness baseline
  - coalesced row work
  - row-shape specialization
  - launch-count reduction
  - level/family specialization
  - learned routing
- Profiling repeatedly changed the direction of development:
  - first away from row-by-row launches
  - then toward family-specific and scheduling-aware kernels
- The final GPU benchmark interface was simplified so the main comparison stayed
  fair and easy to explain.
